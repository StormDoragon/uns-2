Illness & Hardship · Qur’an 21:83

Mercy When Harm Touches the Body

A prayer for illness, fatigue, grief, and the quiet ache of enduring for a long time.

The dua

أَنِّي مَسَّنِيَ الضُّرُّ وَأَنْتَ أَرْحَمُ الرَّاحِمِينَ

Annī massaniyaḍ-ḍurru wa anta arḥamur-rāḥimīn

“Harm has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful.”

Qur’an 21:83

The Relatable Pain

There are trials that arrive loudly, and there are trials that simply stay. The kind that changes your routines, your energy, your relationships, and the way you recognize yourself in the mirror.

People may be sympathetic in the beginning. Later, life moves on around you, while you are still carrying the same pain. You do not always need a speech. Sometimes you only need words honest enough to say, ‘This hurts,’ without losing sight of Allah’s mercy.

The Dark Place

Ayyub عليه السلام is remembered for patience, but his patience was not numbness. He felt the harm. He knew the weight of it. His dua does not hide that reality: أَنِّي مَسَّنِيَ الضُّرُّ — harm has touched me.

Yet he does not make pain the largest thing in the sentence. He places it beside a greater truth: وَأَنْتَ أَرْحَمُ الرَّاحِمِينَ — and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful.

The Turning Point

Notice the adab of the dua. He does not accuse. He does not list demands. He names his wound with restraint, then names Allah’s mercy with certainty. It is a complaint, but it is a complaint carried to the right door.

This is a healing posture for the heart: I am not pretending I am fine, and I am not pretending Allah is distant. Both truths can sit together. Harm has touched me, and Allah’s mercy is still greater than what touched me.

The Closure

Allah answered Ayyub عليه السلام with relief, restoration, and mercy. The dua remains for every believer who feels worn down by something that has stayed too long.

When your body is weak, when your mind is tired, when your sabr feels thin, let this dua carry what you cannot explain. Say it without drama and without despair: the harm is real, but it is not more real than the mercy of Allah.

The takeaway for you

You are allowed to name your pain while still anchoring your hope in Allah’s mercy.

Give the words space. Read them once for meaning, once for need, and once as a quiet return to Allah.
